Stori Home.

Every time a fintech adds a product, the home screen pays the tax. This is how I redesigned Stori's home from a fixed layout into a system that adapts to each user's product mix — tested across three information architectures before we shipped.

§ 01 Context

A home screen under the weight of a growing product suite.

Stori is a venture-backed fintech on a mission to democratize credit access for the 400 million underbanked consumers in Latin America. Operating in Mexico, it has become one of the country's leading digital banks, with more than 2 million applicants for its credit card since launch.

But success created a design problem. What began as a clean, single-product entry point was becoming a crowded surface as Stori layered on new financial products. The home screen — the most-visited surface in the app — was quietly becoming the hardest one to navigate.

§ 02 The challenge

One layout, three competing demands.

With multiple products live and more on the roadmap, the home screen had to do three things at once — and a fixed layout couldn't do all three:

  1. Give every user fast access to the activities they performed most.
  2. Stay clear and easy to navigate, regardless of how much was on screen.
  3. Be flexible enough to absorb whatever product shipped next, without a redesign.

The brief was to redesign the home experience to improve usability while accommodating a growing number of financial products. The real question underneath it: should the home screen even be a single, fixed design at all?

§ 03 Approach

Decide with evidence, not opinion.

The home screen is the most contested surface in any app — everyone has a stake in it. To keep the redesign grounded, I structured the work around three evidence-gathering steps.

01 — Data-driven analysis

I started in the analytics. By analyzing user behavior, I identified the top five activities users performed most frequently — the non-negotiables that any layout had to surface fast.

02 — Prototype development

Rather than designing one "best guess," I built three prototypes, each with a different information architecture. The goal wasn't to fall in love with a layout — it was to create real options to test against each other.

03 — Usability testing

I ran task-based usability testing, where participants completed key tasks across the three prototypes. This let me see — not guess — which structure balanced quick access to frequent tasks against the complexity of a multi-product app.

§ 04 Insights

The testing changed the question.

The usability sessions surfaced three insights that, together, reframed the entire problem:

  1. User preferences varied by product count. What worked for a user with one product actively got in the way of a user with several.
  2. A static layout didn't scale. Any single fixed design was either too sparse for power users or too dense for new ones.
  3. Users wanted both speed and discoverability. Quick access to their most-used actions — but also a clear path to the products they hadn't adopted yet.
"The home screen wasn't one design problem. It was a different problem for every user — depending on how much of Stori they used."

That insight is what moved the project from "redesign the layout" to "design a layout that isn't fixed at all."

§ 05 The solution

A home that adapts to each user.

Instead of a one-size-fits-all screen, I designed a dynamic home layout that adapts based on the number of products a user holds. One system, not one design.

What the system guarantees

  1. Simple, focused navigation for users with fewer products — nothing on screen they don't need yet.
  2. A structured but scalable layout for multi-product users, organized to reduce cognitive overload as the suite grows.
  3. Improved accessibility to frequently used features, anchored by the top-five activities the data identified.

§ 06 Reflection

The first step in an evolving surface.

This redesign was the first approach to a problem Stori will keep solving as it grows. Based on the findings, the team continued iterating — refining the design and adapting it to new business needs, so the home experience could stay seamless and scalable.

What worked

Building three prototypes instead of one. It's tempting to defend a single layout, but on a surface this contested, having real, testable options is what let the decision come from evidence rather than from the loudest voice in the room.

What I'd measure next

This phase proved the concept of an adaptive home. The next phase should prove its impact: time-to-task for the top-five activities, navigation success rate across product counts, and discovery rate for products users haven't adopted yet. That's the instrumentation I'd put in place before the next iteration ships.
